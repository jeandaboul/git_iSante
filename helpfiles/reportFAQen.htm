Haiti EMR

Frequently Asked Questions

 

 

What is the difference between inactive and discontinued patients?

 

"Inactive" patients are defined as follows:


Inactive patients on ART: All patients ever dispensed ART medications at the reporting clinic site with no visits of any kind in the last 90 days. Visits may include clinical evaluation, laboratory, or pharmacy services. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered inactive patients, even if they have not had a visit in the last 90 days.


Inactive patients in palliative care (not on ART): All patients who have never received ART medications at the reporting clinic site with no follow-up visits in the last 180 days. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered inactive patients, even if they have not had a follow-up visit in the last 180 days.


A patient is only counted as "Discontinued" if a discontinuation form has been completed for that patient. "Inactive" patients and "Discontinued" patients represent separate categories, so "Inactive" patients do not also appear on the list of "Discontinued" patients.

 

What is the difference between "Active" patients and patients "At risk of discontinuation"?

 

"Active" patients are defined as follows:


Active patients on ART: All patients ever dispensed ART medications at the reporting clinic site, excluding patients dispensed ART for PMTCT or PEP, with at least one visit of any kind in the last 90 days. Visits may include clinical evaluation, laboratory, or pharmacy services. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered active patients, even if they have had a visit in the last 90 days.


Active patients in palliative care (not on ART): All patients who have never received ART medications at the reporting clinic site who have had at least one visit in the last 180 days. Visits may include clinical evaluation, laboratory, or pharmacy services. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered active patients, even if they have had a visit in the last 180 days.


Patients "At risk of discontinuation" are defined as follows:


Patients on ART at risk of discontinuation: All new or active patients ever dispensed ART medications at the reporting clinic site who have missed their next scheduled prescription pickup by more than 8 days. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered as at risk patients, even if they have missed their next scheduled prescription pickup by more than 8 days.


Patients in palliative care (not on ART) at risk of discontinuation: All new or active patients who have never received ART medications at the reporting clinic site who have missed their next scheduled appointment by more than 30 days. Patients for whom a discontinuation form has been completed are not considered as at risk patients, even if they have missed their next scheduled appointment by more than 30 days.

 

What should be done if a discontinued patient later re-enters care and treatment?

 

When a discontinuation form is completed, the patient record is not erased from the EMR database. All the patient's data remain in the system. Therefore, if a patient re-enters care and treatment, a site data manager may delete their discontinuation form and the clinical team can resume creating new visit records for the patient. At this point, the discontinuation form remains in the system, but with a "flag" as a deleted form. Once the discontinuation form is deleted, the patient will again be recognized in one of the other patient status categories (Active, At risk of discontinuation, Inactive). It is possible to enter another discontinuation form at a later date, if the patient is discontinued for a second time.

 

 

Among the Quality of Care reports, what is the difference between the Reminder reports (Appointment Reminders, Lab Test Reminders, and Care Reminders) and the Indicator reports (Appropriate Lab Test Indicators, Appropriate Treatment Indicators)?

 

The Reminder reports are designed for prospective use – to look ahead to what is needed in the care of patients. These reports provide lists of patients who should have specific follow up, based upon what happened (or did not happen) in their earlier visits.

 

The Indicator reports are designed for retrospective use – to look backwards at the patterns of care provided over time in the past. These indicators serve as measures of the quality of patient care and can help clinic leaders to identify areas to target for improved performance.

 

How should the Quality of Care reports be interpreted?

 

These reports should be used to look for trends over time in the indicators. People using the reports should be aware to interpret the indicators for the current and previous calendar periods with caution. The reports on ART enrollment, Cotrimoxizole prophylaxis, and TB treatment all use criteria that patients should be enrolled on the appropriate medications within 3 months of meeting the medical eligibility criteria. Therefore, the report indicators can only be considered "firm" 3 months after the time window of interest. For example, if one runs a report in January, 2007 on ART enrollment, Cotrimoxizole initiation, or TB treatment initiation, it is possible that those patients who met the respective eligibility criteria in December 2006 might not start the medication until after January, 2007. If you run this same report in April, 2007, you can interpret the data for the time window of July-December 2006 as "firm." At this point you will know that any patients who did not receive the recommended medication by the end March 2006, were truly falling outside the standard of care (that appropriate treatment or prophylaxis be initiated within 3 months of meeting the medical eligibility criteria).
